# StrideMind Voice & Tone

> A duplicate of this guide lives in the website repo (`stridemind-legal/design/voice.md`)
> so the website and the app speak with one voice. If this file changes, update that copy too.

The canonical guide for every word the user reads. Read this before writing or
editing any user-facing string (UI copy, verdicts, the exported report, notification
text, error messages).

## The voice

Every word should sound like the same person who narrates the walking tracks:
**calm, kind, explanatory.** Picture that narrator standing next to a 60-year-old,
saying this out loud. If they wouldn't say it that way, rewrite it.

**Audience:** Active Agers 55+, many thinking about their cognitive health for the
first time. Results feel high-stakes. Clinical, terse, or cute copy leaves them
anxious or talked-down-to.

**Target feelings:** proud, capable, dignified, calm, in control.
**Anti-targets:** clinical, sterile, graded, gamified, infantilized, billboard-cheery.

## Hard rules

1. **Complete, natural sentences. Never fragments.** This is the rule most often
   broken. Punchy headlines like "As consistent as ever.", "Same score, same sharp.",
   "Holding steady.", "No drift since you started." read like marketing billboards,
   not like a person talking. Headlines can and should be whole sentences.
2. **Explain the meaning, not just the number.** Always answer "what does this mean
   for me?"
3. **Be warm and reassuring around results.** Honest, never alarming.
4. **Speak to the user as "you."** A coach beside them, not a chart describing them.
5. **No jargon.** Never use IIV, DTC, d-prime, false alarm rate, n-back, dual-task
   cost, or raw statistics. Translate using the table below.
6. **No em dashes.** Use commas and periods.
7. **Forgiving, never punishing.** A missed day or down week is ordinary (health
   reasons). A down result is a neutral reference, never a failure verdict. Only the
   up direction gets a celebratory arrow or green. (See the forgiving-progress
   principle: consistency over streaks.)
8. **Avoid "steady" as a verdict word.** The user dislikes it. The consistency
   metric (statistically IIV) is named **"consistency"** across Progress, not
   "steadiness."

## The maintenance verdict (the calibration target)

When a measure hasn't meaningfully changed, frame it as a real, dignified result, not
a plateau to worry about. The pattern that lands: **name what happened plainly, then
reassure it's a genuine result,** often closing with "exactly what you want" / "what
staying sharp looks like" / "what you want to see."

These are the approved, user-chosen examples. Match this register:

- **Go/No-Go (timing consistency held):**
  "Your reactions have stayed consistent."
  "There's been no significant changes since your first check-ins, and for an ongoing habit,
  maintaining it is exactly what you want."

- **Working memory (score held on a hard test):**
  "You've held onto your score."
  "This test is hard on purpose. Repeating a score this close over time is exactly
  what staying sharp looks like."

- **Exported report (WM line):**
  "There's been no significant changes since the start (+N points). Maintaining a demanding score
  over many months is what you want to see."

## Metric translation table

Never show the raw term. Translate:

| Internal term | Say instead |
|---|---|
| IIV / consistency | "how consistent your timing was" / "your focus held" |
| mean RT / reaction speed | "how quickly you reacted" |
| dual-task cost (DTC) | "the extra mental effort of thinking while you walk" |
| false alarms | "the times you tapped when you didn't mean to" |
| hit rate | "how many you caught" |
| 2-back / working memory | "holding a few things in mind at once" |
| seated baseline | "your starting point; how you do sitting still" |

## Never use

- "we kept it" / "we're keeping the faster one" (transactional, robotic)
- A raw metric with no explanation
- Medical or statistical jargon
- Fragment copy to save space
- Alarming or clinical framing
- The word "steady" in a verdict

## Before / after

- Bad: "Previous 812ms, this attempt 749ms, kept the faster one."
  Good: "You reacted a bit quicker this time than last, so that's the number you're
  seeing."
- Bad: "Lower IIV indicates improved attentional consistency."
  Good: "A lower number means your timing was more consistent, so your focus held
  better."
- Bad: "DTC +62ms. Within normal range."
  Good: "Your reactions were about 62 milliseconds slower while walking than sitting.
  That gap is completely normal. It's the mental effort of doing two things at once,
  and it's exactly what these sessions help shrink over time."
- Bad: "No data. Complete a session."
  Good: "Once you finish your first session, you'll start to see your progress here."
- Bad (fragment headline): "Holding steady."
  Good (full sentence): "Your reactions have stayed consistent."

## The test

Read it back and ask: **would the voice from the walking tracks say this to a
60-year-old standing next to them?** If no, rewrite.
